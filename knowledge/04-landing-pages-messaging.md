# Landing Pages & Conversion

## Overview

Landing pages and homepages are the proof-of-concept for your positioning. A well-structured homepage answers six critical questions—what is the product, who is it for, what problem does it solve, what can users do with it, what are the main benefits, and can they trust the company. The goal is not to impress but to guide visitors toward action in under 90 seconds.

## Homepage Structure

### The 10-Section Blueprint

Every high-converting SaaS homepage contains these sections in order:

1. **Sticky Navigation** – Primary CTA visible at all times
2. **Hero Section** – Problem/solution statement, max 6 words per line, 2 lines total; include "what it does," "who it's for," "why choose it"
3. **Social Proof (First)** – Logos with context (e.g., "Top GTM teams use..."), customer type identification
4. **Problem Section** – Articulate pain points before presenting solution
5. **Solution + Value Propositions** – 3–5 differentiated outcomes with proof points; combine benefits with feature screenshots
6. **Use-Case or Segment Routing** – Optional: "Selling to GTM teams? See here. Selling to founders? See here."
7. **How-It-Works Diagram** – Optional: walkthrough or flow diagram with clear context
8. **Social Proof (Second)** – 7–21 elements minimum; quotes, metrics, usage stats, review ratings (4.1–4.7 star range)
9. **CTA Bar** – Restate core value proposition above footer
10. **Footer** – Link to pricing, resources, company info

**Critical Principle**: "Your homepage isn't about you. It's about THEM—your ideal customer. Their problems. Their desires. Their language."

### Best Practices

- **Hero lean rule**: Excessive copy reduces conversions; keep it scannable
- **Social proof specificity**: "Top GTM teams love X" outperforms "Companies love us"
- **Proof hierarchy**: Product screenshots > illustrations; authentic testimonials > stock photos
- **Layout simplicity**: Scannable, linear layouts convert better than complex animations, carousels, or jarring UX
- **Content-first design**: Build messaging framework before designing pixels
- **Mobile-first responsive**: Tablet/laptop-first viewport (350px+ min width)

## Pricing Page

### Structure

Every pricing page needs four core sections:

1. **Hero** – Communicate how customers start, why different plan types exist, how product scales with growth
2. **Pricing Menu** – 3 plans (ideal), max 5 to avoid cognitive overload; clearly display monthly cost even for annual plans
3. **Feature Comparison Table** – Categorized by features, grouped logically, expandable sections with tooltips
4. **FAQ** – 8–12 questions max (accordion format); address trial availability, refund policy, data security, payment methods

### Plan Architecture

Each plan requires these six elements:

- **Modality**: How charged (per seat, usage-based, flat fee, hybrid)
- **Metrics**: Unit or value metric (e.g., "per $1M revenue processed")
- **Value Ladder**: Features scale across tiers to justify tier progression
- **Packaging**: Tier names (Free, Pro, Business, Enterprise)
- **Price Display**: Show monthly cost on all plans; include annual option with savings indicator
- **CTA**: Clear, specific call-to-action per tier

### Best Practices

- **Decision window**: Visitors should decide which plan fits within 30 seconds
- **Feature progression**: Waterfall-style visibility showing feature additions per tier
- **Recommendation label**: Mark high-value tier as "Recommended" or "Most Popular"
- **Billing toggle**: Monthly/annual switch visible above pricing menu
- **Value metric clarity**: Define each metric in FAQ; avoid internal jargon
- **Transparency**: Public pricing is now a competitive advantage (especially for self-serve)
- **Upsell context**: Use in-product feature usage to trigger upgrade prompts in high-friction areas
- **Feature demoting**: Convert underutilized features (<30% adoption per plan) to add-ons
- **No friction**: Never use pop-ups, newsletters signups, or hidden pricing

### Benchmarks

| Metric | Target | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Freemium free-to-paid | 3–5% | Lower bound; reverse trial improves this |
| Trial free-to-paid | 10–15% | Better conversion than freemium alone |
| Pricing-to-checkout | 25% | Self-serve, in-app |
| Checkout-to-order | 50%+ | Required for viable acquisition |
| Pricing page visits (freemium) | 25% | % of signups who visit pricing |
| Decision time | 30 seconds | Visitor should choose tier in this window |

## Product Pages

Build three complementary page types as your product grows:

### Feature Pages

Structure: Hero + social proof + benefits + features + how-it-works + case studies + FAQ + CTA

- Clear headline explaining what, who benefits, and why it matters
- Feature details paired with outcome benefits (not just "does X," but "enables Y result")
- Screenshots of feature in context, labeled with key capabilities
- Link to related use cases and customer segments using this feature

### Use-Case Pages

Structure: Pillar overview listing all use cases; sub-pages for each use case

- Create overview/index page linking to all use cases
- Each use case page shows: problem → features powering solution → use-case-specific case studies
- "Additional resources" section linking relevant features, blog articles, templates

### Customer-Segment Pages

Structure: Segment type in H1 + segment-specific content

- Industry-relevant customer logos (mix of recognizable brands with emerging winners)
- Segment-specific use cases and relevant feature subsets only
- Before-and-after scenarios showing typical customer journey
- Case studies from similar companies (same vertical, similar size)
- Segment-specific CTAs and value propositions

### Site Structure Template

```
/features
  /feature-name
/usecases
  /usecase-name
/solutions (or /teams or /industries)
  /saas-companies
  /agencies
  /ecommerce
```

### Cross-Linking Strategy

- Features link to relevant use cases and segments
- Use cases link to feature pages and customer segment pages
- All pages include "explore other features/use cases" navigation
- Distinct CTAs per page type (e.g., "See how features work" vs. "See customer story")

## Social Proof

### Types

Ordered by conversion impact:

1. **Video testimonials** – Highest authenticity and engagement; 30-minute interview → 30+ social clips
2. **Case studies with metrics** – Quantified before/after results; "grew revenue 40%" vs. "works great"
3. **Customer logos with context** – "500+ companies" + specific industry/use-case labels
4. **Star ratings** – 4.1–4.7 range most credible (5/5 with few reviews appears fake)
5. **Quotes with photos** – Company name, role, photo (higher trust than anonymous)
6. **Usage stats** – "10 million API calls/month," "trusted by 50% of Fortune 500," deployment numbers
7. **Customer-generated content** – Organic social posts, product screenshots, unprompted mentions

### Collection System

Weekly KPI: "# of testimonials generated"

1. **Build centralized form** (Notion template provided; fields: customer name, company, quote, context, photo/video, metric)
2. **Request in post-purchase flow** – Embed link in welcome email; offer to draft testimonial for approval (low friction)
3. **Mine organic mentions** – Scan LinkedIn, Reddit, Slack, Discord, support tickets for unprompted feedback
4. **Hand-select high-impact customers** – For SLG products, build 2–3 detailed case studies covering different use cases/industries/benefits
5. **Systematize interviews** – Document questions covering: challenges faced, failed solutions, implementation experience, results, recommendation likelihood

### Repurposing Strategy

**One 30-minute interview → Multiple formats**:
- 1 written case study (2–3 pages)
- 30+ short social clips (15–30 seconds each)
- 5–10 quote cards (visual + text)
- 2–3 video testimonials (standalone clips)

**Distribution channels**: Website (homepage, product pages, pricing), sales decks, LinkedIn posts, ad creatives, investor decks, blog articles, email sequences

### Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Vague praise ("Great product!" "5/5 stars!") without context
- ❌ Generic testimonials lacking customer/company identification
- ❌ Purchased reviews or incentivized feedback ("rate us, win $50")
- ❌ Overly polished/produced materials lacking authenticity
- ❌ Missing verification elements (screenshot, quote attribution)
- ❌ Gated case studies (friction destroys reach; make all ungated)

## Case Studies

### Storytelling Arc

Problem (Situation A) → Pain Points → Failed Solutions → Your Product → Resolution → Quantified Results (Situation B)

### Essential Sections

- **Customer story and background** – Company, size, industry, role
- **Specific challenges pre-solution** – Quantified pain (lost revenue, wasted time, manual work)
- **How your product addressed problems** – Feature → customer benefit mapping
- **Implementation details** – Setup time, learning curve, timeline to value
- **Quantifiable results** – Metrics with graphs (revenue increase, time saved, feature adoption)
- **Customer quote + photo** – Direct, in-voice statement recommending solution
- **CTA** – Link to trial, demo, or contact sales

### Modern Formats

| Format | Best For | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Written (ungated) | Data-driven audiences | 2–3 pages, PDF or web page |
| Wall of Love | Social proof density | 20+ short quotes/testimonials on single page |
| Video | Engagement and authenticity | 3–5 minute customer story; interview format |
| Quote Library | Internal distribution | Notion database, searchable by use case/industry |

### Getting Started

- Start with 2–3 beta/pilot customers; don't wait for massive customer base
- Interview question framework: challenges, solution discovery, implementation, results, likelihood to recommend
- Offer to draft case study for approval (low friction for busy customers)
- Ungated distribution (no email capture wall) maximizes reach and credibility

### Pricing Page Checklist

- [ ] Hero section sets context for pricing strategy
- [ ] 3 plans named simply (Free, Pro, Enterprise) or tied to user type
- [ ] Each plan has 2+ upgrade drivers justifying tier progression
- [ ] Monthly cost displayed prominently; annual option with savings ≥20%
- [ ] "Recommended" or "Most Popular" label on high-value tier
- [ ] Feature table grouped by category (Collaboration, Automation, Analytics)
- [ ] Expandable rows for complex features; tooltips explaining unclear terms
- [ ] FAQ covers: free trial duration, refund policy, data security, payment methods, feature availability
- [ ] CTA per plan and in footer; avoid generic "Sign Up" (use "Start Free," "Upgrade Now")
- [ ] No pop-ups, carousels, or newsletter signups interrupting pricing page

### Case Study Template (Google Doc)

```
# [Customer Name] Case Study

## The Situation
[2–3 sentences: company, industry, team size, current state]

## The Challenge
[Describe 2–3 specific pain points with quantification]

- This cost them $Z in lost/wasted resources

## The Solution
[How your product addresses each pain point]

"[Direct, authentic quote from stakeholder]"

## Next Steps
[CTA: Try free, Request demo, etc.]
```

## Benchmarks

| Metric | Target | Context |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Homepage hero clarity | 90%+ comprehension | Test via UserTesting in <10 seconds |
| Bounce rate | <50% | Industry average 40–60%; improve with clarity |
| Scroll depth | 70%+ viewing features | Ensure content below hero is visible |
| Social proof elements (homepage) | 7–21 minimum | Higher-converting pages cluster here |
| Star rating credibility | 4.1–4.7 range | 5/5 with few reviews signals inauthenticity |
| Pricing decision time | 30 seconds | Visitor should choose plan in this window |
| Case study impact | 2x faster closes | Leads citing case studies convert faster |
| Testimonial specificity | 100% have context | Company, role, metric, or use case identified |

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Starting homepage with features instead of customer problems
- ❌ Homepage serving as exhaustive feature documentation
- ❌ Too many CTAs in hero section (confuses intent)
- ❌ Carousels, complex animations, or jarring UX that distracts
- ❌ Pricing pages with 5+ plans (cognitive overload)
- ❌ Generic pricing page hero without context-setting
- ❌ Feature grids without categorization or logical grouping
- ❌ Using tabs to hide product offerings (users rarely click secondary tabs)
- ❌ Hidden pricing pages (signals weakness; transparency now expected)
- ❌ Vague social proof ("Great product!" without metrics or context)
- ❌ Gated case studies (friction reduces reach; always ungated)
- ❌ Identical product page structure across all features/use cases/segments
