# Pricing Strategy

## Overview
Pricing is your highest-leverage revenue lever—a 1% improvement yields 4x more revenue impact than equivalent acquisition gains. The goal: capture 15-25% of the customer value you create, tested with real prospects before product launch, then continuously optimize through A/B testing.

## Frameworks

### Value Transfer Model
Your product delivers value to customers; pricing captures that value back. Typical capture: 10-30% of annual value added in SaaS. Calculate by quantifying customer benefit (revenue gained, hours saved, cost reduced) and price at 15-25% of that annual value.

### 5-Step Implementation Process
1. Understand customer pain and quantify it (hours, cost savings, revenue impact)
2. Adopt value-based pricing (charge for customer value delivered, not cost-plus)
3. Select a value metric (per-user, per-transaction, per-booking, custom)
4. Determine price range ($10, $100, $1,000 level matters; exact price less critical initially)
5. Design packaging and terms (tiers, discounts, trial structure)

### Value Metric Selection
Must be: (1) easy to understand, (2) aligned with customer value, (3) scalable. Start simple.

**Common metrics:**
- Per user/seat
- Per transaction/booking
- Per revenue generated
- Per storage (GB)
- Per subscriber/contact
- Per action (e.g., "zaps" for Zapier)

**Anti-pattern:** Starting with multiple complex metrics. Single metric works better.

### Pricing Psychology
- **Price Anchoring & Decoy Effect**: Highlight your recommended tier prominently. Include a third, less-attractive option to make standard packages appear more valuable.
- **Isolation Effect**: Use badges/distinct styling on preferred tier to boost conversion.
- **Loss Aversion Barrier**: Founders hesitate to adjust pricing due to fear of customer rejection. Solution: price confidently based on value; grandfather existing customers while new ones pay market rate.

## Playbook

### Pre-Launch Validation
1. Conduct structured conversations with 10-15 prospects asking about acceptable price ranges
2. Presell or offer pilot access (not surveys) to test willingness-to-pay before completion
3. Frame discussions around perceived value, not cost
4. Identify price sensitivity variations by segment (use case, team size, industry)

### Setting Your Initial Price
1. Quantify value per customer (revenue gained, hours saved, cost eliminated annually)
2. Calculate 15-25% of that as your target price
3. Validate willingness-to-pay across segments through prospect conversations
4. Set price range level ($10/mo vs. $100/mo vs. $1,000/mo) before exact pricing
5. Design 3-4 tiers maximum; limit to avoid choice paralysis

### Testing & Iteration
1. Run A/B tests on pricing pages simultaneously (tier positioning, feature sets, anchor language, discount structures)
2. Review pricing minimum quarterly; best-in-class test continuously
3. Collect churn reasons and lost deal reasons to identify pricing sensitivity
4. Monitor expansion revenue potential per customer segment
5. Create pricing moodboard: competitor screenshots (dated), changes over time, variations, industry benchmarks—update quarterly

### Price Increases
1. Announce 3+ months in advance
2. Tie explicitly to new features or market-wide cost increases
3. Calculate revenue impact on existing customers before applying changes
4. Consider grandfathering legacy customers on old pricing for 12 months (builds goodwill)

## Pricing Models

### Freemium
When to use: When you have 50+ million active users or strong viral loops (most founders overestimate fit). **Anti-pattern:** Assuming freemium works without massive scale.

### Free Trial (Reverse Model)
When to use: SaaS, B2B tooling. Grant full feature access during trial; at expiration, downgrade to limited free tier or require conversion. Outperforms limited-feature trials.

### Usage-Based (Per-Transaction, Per-Booking, Per-Action)
When to use: Products with variable customer value (marketplaces, APIs, transaction platforms). Align metric directly to customer ROI.

### Per-Seat (Per-User)
When to use: Collaboration tools, team software. Simple to understand and scale with team growth.

### Flat-Rate
When to use: When value doesn't vary significantly by usage or team size. Simplest to sell; limits flexibility.

### Value-Based + Annual Discount
When to use: B2B SaaS with predictable multi-year usage. Offer 10-20% discount for annual upfront (cap at 20% to preserve margins). Use "discount" as lever to request testimonials, case studies, or pilot participation.

## Templates

### Free Trial Onboarding Checklist
- [ ] Sign-up page displays testimonials, logos, core capability, "no credit card required"
- [ ] Onboarding survey captures: usecase, role, team size, discovery channel
- [ ] In-product tours and contextual feature guides (not static docs)
- [ ] Upgrade CTA always visible; trial end date prominently shown
- [ ] Viral loops for team invitations enabled

### Pricing Conversation Script
*"Can you help me understand how your team currently solves [problem]? How much time does it take per week/month? What does that cost you annually in salary or opportunity cost? Would you consider a tool worth $[price] if it cut that time in half?"*

### Price Testing Plan
| Test | Variants | Duration | Success Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier positioning | Recommended badge (standard vs. premium vs. best value) | 2-4 weeks | Conversion rate to each tier |
| Feature sets | Include feature X in mid-tier vs. higher tier | 2-4 weeks | Mid-tier conversion; expansion revenue |
| Discount | 10% vs. 20% annual discount | 2-4 weeks | Annual plan attach rate; revenue |
| Anchor language | "Popular" vs. "Most powerful" vs. "Best for teams" | 2-4 weeks | Tier distribution shift |

### Annual Plan Pitch
*"Switching to annual saves your team $[amount] and gives you predictable budgeting. Plus, we'll [offer longer trial / early access to new features / monthly check-in calls]. The commitment signals to us that you're serious—we prioritize those accounts for support."*

## Benchmarks

| Metric | Target | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Value capture | 15-25% of annual value | Calculate after quantifying customer benefit |
| Competition clustering | 20-30% variance from established players | New entrants typically land here due to anchoring |
| Revenue leverage | 1% monetization gain = 4x impact of acquisition optimization | Pricing is your highest-lever metric |
| Tier count | 3-4 maximum | Avoids choice paralysis; improves conversion |
| Annual discount | 10-20% | Cap at 20%; use as lever for data/testimonials |
| Price review cadence | Minimum quarterly; continuous for leaders | Tied to feature releases and market changes |
| Discount redemption | Exchange discounts for testimonials, case studies, pilot participation | Discounts alone destroy margin and mindset |
| Churn signal tracking | Monitor pricing objections in lost deals and churn reasons | Indicates willingness-to-pay misalignment |

## Anti-Patterns

- **Race to the bottom**: Only one competitor can sustain lowest pricing; requires massive capital. Margins collapse. Avoid.
- **Cost-plus thinking**: Costs don't determine price; customer value does. Ignore internal costs when setting pricing.
- **Assumption-based pricing**: Setting prices without direct customer input. Always test willingness-to-pay first.
- **One-size-fits-all**: Ignoring segment variation (use case, team size, industry). Different segments have different valuations.
- **Set-and-forget**: Pricing static after launch ignores feature improvements, cost inflation, market changes. Review quarterly minimum.
- **Founder-anchored underbilling**: Fixing prices to early customers creates dependency, blocks roadmap, suffocates margins. Price confidently from start.
- **Complex value metrics**: Multiple bundled metrics confuse customers and lower conversion. Start with one.
- **Shared pricing ownership**: No single owner = no accountability = stagnation. Assign clear ownership.
- **Hidden pricing (B2B)**: Lack of transparency creates friction. Always show pricing except enterprise deals.
- **Freemium without scale**: Requires 50+ million active users to work. Most founders overestimate their gravity.
- **High sign-up bounce + poor time-to-value**: Trial fails if users can't experience value quickly. Prioritize onboarding.
- **Microeconomic oversimplification**: Supply-demand curves don't apply predictably to tech. Pricing psychology matters more.
